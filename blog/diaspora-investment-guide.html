How to Invest in Nigerian Real Estate from Abroad: The 2026 Diaspora Guide

By Blessing Collins (Eva) 📅 April 12, 2026 ⏱️ 12 min read

If you're reading this from London, Houston, Toronto, Dubai, or anywhere else in the diaspora — and you've been thinking about buying property back home — you're not alone. Remittances from Nigerians abroad crossed $20 billion in 2024, and a rapidly growing share of that is going into real estate.

But here's the uncomfortable truth: more diaspora Nigerians get scammed in real estate than in any other sector. Fake land sales, duplicate titles, "omo onile" demanding endless fees, and properties built on government land — I've seen all of them.

This guide is the playbook I wish every diaspora client had before they started. Read it end to end, and you'll save yourself years of frustration and millions in losses.

Why Nigerian Real Estate Still Wins in 2026

Despite currency volatility, Nigerian real estate remains one of the strongest stores of value available to diaspora investors. Here's why:

  • Land supply is fixed. Lagos is running out of dry land. Ibadan and the Ogun corridor are where Lagos was 15 years ago — prices have room to run.
  • Naira depreciation works in your favor. When you're earning in dollars, pounds, or euros, every year the naira weakens, your purchasing power in Nigerian real estate grows.
  • Rental yields beat most Western markets. Serviced apartments in Ikoyi and Lekki deliver 6-9% gross yields, often with furnished premiums.
  • Agricultural real estate is an untapped frontier. Farmland partnerships can return 20-40% annually through dual upside of land appreciation plus harvest revenue.

Step 1: Get Your Legal Foundation Right

Before you even look at a listing, get your legal structure sorted. This is the single biggest mistake diaspora investors make — buying first, structuring later.

Option A: Buy in Your Personal Name

Simple. Fast. Fine for 1-2 properties. You sign a Power of Attorney (POA) with a trusted attorney in Nigeria so they can act on your behalf for signing, registration, and routine management.

Option B: Buy Through a Nigerian Limited Company

Best for portfolio builders. Easier resale, cleaner tax treatment, and you can add family members as directors. Set up costs are manageable — ₦250,000-₦500,000 including CAC registration.

Option C: Buy Through an Investment Trust or Family Office

For high-net-worth diaspora clients building multi-generational wealth. More setup, but better for estate planning and asset protection.

The right structure depends on your goals, your family situation, and your exit strategy. Don't default to "personal name" just because it's easy.

Step 2: Understand Nigerian Land Titles (the Non-Negotiable Basics)

If you remember nothing else from this guide, remember this: not all land titles are equal. Here's the hierarchy, from strongest to weakest:

Certificate of Occupancy (C of O)

The gold standard. Issued by the state government. Confirms you have exclusive legal rights to the land for 99 years. Non-negotiable for serious investment.

Governor's Consent

Required when you're buying land that already has a C of O from a previous owner. Without Governor's Consent, the transfer is legally incomplete. Skipping this step is the #1 cause of title disputes.

Deed of Assignment

The legal document that transfers ownership from seller to buyer. Must be registered at the state land registry.

Excision & Gazette

When buying in an area that was formerly under government acquisition (common in Lagos), the land must have been excised — officially released back to the community. The gazette is the published government record confirming this.

Survey Plan

Defines the exact boundaries. Should match what's on the ground. A registered surveyor must confirm no overlap with existing titles.

Step 3: Remote Due Diligence — The Eva Method

You can't fly in for every viewing. Here's the remote due diligence protocol I use with every diaspora client:

  1. Video walkthrough of the property at different times of day (morning and evening — to check lighting, noise, and security).
  2. Drone footage showing access roads, neighboring properties, and proximity to main roads or infrastructure.
  3. Title verification by an independent lawyer (not the seller's lawyer) at the state lands registry.
  4. Survey plan comparison — cross-check boundaries against government records.
  5. Community due diligence — speak to neighbors, local chiefs, and any CDA (Community Development Association) to confirm there are no land disputes or pending claims.
  6. Escrow payment — never, ever pay the seller directly. Funds go into a lawyer-managed escrow account and only release when documentation is confirmed.

Step 4: The 5 Most Common Diaspora Scams (and How to Spot Them)

Scam #1: The "Family Land" Pressure Sale

A "relative" calls: "Uncle has land for sale, family price, must pay this week." The land either doesn't belong to them, or it has 14 other co-owners you've never met. Fix: Never buy family land without written consent from all named heirs.

Scam #2: The Double Sale

Same plot is sold to 3-5 buyers, each given valid-looking documents. Fix: Only buy land where you can see the physical registry entry and survey plan cross-check.

Scam #3: Government Acquired Land

Beautiful estate, attractive price — but the land is under government acquisition. You'll lose it when the government develops it. Fix: Confirm excision & gazette before any payment.

Scam #4: The "Omo Onile" Trap

You buy land, begin construction, and suddenly families show up demanding "foundation fee," "welcome fee," "roofing fee." Fix: Buy in structured, gated estates with a single clear seller — the estate developer absorbs the omo onile complexity.

Scam #5: The Fake Agent

Someone claiming to represent a popular developer, running deep discounts, accepting personal transfers. Fix: Only deal with agents who have verifiable client history, physical offices or long-established online presence, and whose payment flows go through corporate accounts.

Step 5: Tax & Repatriation Considerations

A few things to know before you wire funds:

  • Inflow documentation: Transfer funds through your Nigerian bank with proper "Certificate of Capital Importation" (CCI). This makes future repatriation of rental income or sale proceeds smooth.
  • Property taxes: State governments are increasingly enforcing land use charges and tenement rates. Budget ₦50,000-₦500,000/year depending on property size and location.
  • Capital gains tax: 10% on profits when you sell, though exemptions exist for primary residences held 5+ years.
  • Home country tax: Check whether your country of residence has rules on foreign real estate holdings. US citizens have FBAR/FATCA reporting obligations above certain thresholds.

Step 6: Plan Your Exit Before You Buy

The best diaspora investors don't ask "how much will this cost?" — they ask "how will I sell this in 5-10 years?" Before buying, answer:

  • Who is the most likely buyer when I'm ready to exit? (Young families? Developers? Commercial tenants?)
  • Will this location still be attractive when I'm ready to sell?
  • If I hold it for 10 years, what's the realistic exit price given appreciation trends?
  • What's my Plan B if I can't sell — can it generate rental or agricultural income?

Eva's Diaspora Checklist — Copy This

  • ☐ Legal structure decided (personal, company, trust)
  • ☐ Trusted Nigerian lawyer appointed
  • ☐ Power of Attorney drafted and notarized
  • ☐ Target locations shortlisted with ROI data
  • ☐ Budget set in BOTH naira and your home currency
  • ☐ Exit timeline clearly defined
  • ☐ Emergency contact / local point person agreed
  • ☐ Escrow arrangement confirmed before any payment
  • ☐ Title verification completed independently
  • ☐ Insurance and property management plan in place
