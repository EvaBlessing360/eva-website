5 Red Flags to Avoid When Buying Land in Nigeria

By Blessing Collins (Eva) 📅 February 22, 2026 ⏱️ 8 min read

I've spent years watching good, smart, cautious people lose millions to bad land deals. It's almost never because they were careless — it's because nobody told them exactly what to look for.

Here are the five red flags that show up in almost every failed land purchase. Memorize them. Avoid them. Save yourself years of regret.

Red Flag #1: "Urgent, Family Price, Pay Today"

Real estate does not have Black Friday sales. If a seller, agent, or "uncle" is pressuring you to transfer money today because "the price goes up tomorrow" or "another buyer is ready" — stop. Every single time.

Why this flag exists: Legitimate sellers know that title verification, survey confirmation, and documentation take 2-4 weeks minimum. Any deal that can't survive a 2-week delay is a deal designed to prevent you from doing due diligence.

What to do instead:

  • Agree a standard 14-day due diligence window in writing before any payment.
  • Pay a token deposit (max ₦500,000) into escrow — not to the seller's personal account.
  • If they refuse to wait, walk away. Period.

Red Flag #2: The "Original Copy" They Won't Let You Photograph

You ask to see the Certificate of Occupancy. They show you a pristine folder, let you look at it briefly, and then refuse to give you a photocopy or let you take a photo. "Security reasons." "The lawyer has the original."

Why this flag exists: Real C of Os are registered at the state lands registry. You can walk in, pay a small fee, and request a certified true copy. Anyone who won't let you verify it independently is hiding something.

What to do instead:

  • Request a photocopy of all title documents at first viewing.
  • Independently verify at the state lands registry — not through the seller's lawyer.
  • Cross-check the Registration, Page, and Volume (RPV) numbers on file.

Red Flag #3: Survey Plan Doesn't Match the Ground

The survey plan shows a rectangular plot with clear beacon numbers. You visit the site and the boundaries don't match — there's a drainage canal where the plan shows open land, or the neighboring building overlaps what's supposed to be your plot.

Why this flag exists: Either the survey is fake, or the land has boundary disputes with neighbors, or it was surveyed incorrectly originally. Any of these become your problem after you pay.

What to do instead:

  • Always visit the land with a registered surveyor who can verify beacons on the ground.
  • Request a "charting" from the state surveyor-general's office to confirm no overlap with existing titles.
  • Take photos with GPS coordinates at each corner and compare to the survey plan.

Red Flag #4: The Land Is in a Government Acquisition Zone

The price is unusually attractive. The area is beautiful. The seller has what looks like a Deed of Assignment. But nobody mentions excision. Nobody mentions gazette.

Why this flag exists: In Lagos especially, vast swathes of land are under government acquisition. Without formal excision (government releasing it back to the community) followed by a gazette publication, any "sale" of that land is legally void. Build on it and the government can come knocking 10 years later. It happens regularly.

What to do instead:

  • Ask explicitly for excision status — in writing.
  • Request the gazette number and confirm it with the Ministry of Physical Planning.
  • If the land doesn't have excision + gazette, treat it as "you don't own this land" — regardless of what the seller claims.

Red Flag #5: The Seller Isn't the Owner (And They Won't Explain)

The title is in someone else's name. They explain: "Oh, the owner is abroad, I have Power of Attorney." Or: "The land belonged to my late grandfather — we all own it together." Or: "I'm selling on behalf of my boss."

Why this flag exists: Either the POA is fake, or the "collective family" is missing 4 disputing siblings, or the "boss" doesn't exist. You'll get contested claims immediately after you pay.

What to do instead:

  • If there's a POA — verify it was notarized, check the expiry, and contact the actual owner directly to confirm.
  • If it's family land — require signatures from all living heirs, in front of a lawyer, with identification.
  • Never buy where the chain of ownership is unclear. Walk away.

Eva's 4-Step Verification Checklist Before Any Payment

  1. Title verification at the state lands registry (independent lawyer, not seller's lawyer).
  2. Physical survey with a registered surveyor, matched to the survey plan and charting.
  3. Excision & gazette confirmation at the relevant state ministry.
  4. Community due diligence — neighbors, CDA, local chief — to confirm no disputes or pending claims.

Total time: 2-4 weeks. Total cost: ₦150,000-₦500,000 depending on location and land size. This is cheap insurance compared to losing ₦20M+ to a bad deal.

My rule is simple: if the seller cannot survive 2 weeks of due diligence, they never intended for you to do any.
