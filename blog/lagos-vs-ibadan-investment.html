Lagos vs. Ibadan: Where Should You Invest in 2026?

By Blessing Collins (Eva) 📅 April 12, 2026 ⏱️ 9 min read

"Eva, where should I put my first ₦20M — Lagos or Ibadan?"

I get a version of this question almost every week — from clients in Houston, London, Toronto, and from Nigerians who finally have capital ready to deploy. And my honest answer is always the same: it depends on your goals, your timeline, and your tolerance for noise.

Lagos and Ibadan are the two corridors I market most actively, and they couldn't be more different. One is loud, fast, and unforgiving. The other is patient, affordable, and deceptively rewarding. Let me show you the data — and tell you what I'd actually do with your money.

Lagos: The High-Ticket, High-Upside Play

Lagos is Nigeria's commercial capital. It's the benchmark every other city is measured against, and — for now — it's also the most expensive place on the map.

What makes Lagos attractive

  • Liquidity. Properties in Lekki, Ikoyi, and Victoria Island can be resold in weeks, not months. If you ever need to exit, Lagos is the easiest market to do it in.
  • Rental demand. Young professionals, expats, and corporate tenants keep occupancy high — particularly for one- to three-bedroom serviced apartments and short-lets.
  • Infrastructure trajectory. The Lekki–Epe corridor, the Lekki Deep Sea Port, the Fourth Mainland Bridge plans, and the Lagos–Ibadan expressway upgrade keep pulling values up the curve.
  • Dollar-denominated income. Diaspora visitors, returning Nigerians, and expat workers will pay in dollars (or dollar-priced naira) for well-finished short-lets in Ikoyi, VI, and Lekki Phase 1. That's the engine behind my short-let portfolio.

The risks

  • Ticket size. Serious entry starts around ₦15M, and a build-ready plot in a respectable estate often clears ₦35M before you've laid a single block.
  • Title complexity. Lagos has the country's most complicated land story — omo onile, government acquisition, Epe excision, family land disputes. This is where due diligence isn't optional.
  • Flood and topography. Some of Lekki Phase 1, Ajah, and parts of Ikoyi flood. Cheap plots are sometimes cheap for a reason. If you don't know the soil and the elevation, you're gambling.
  • Service charge creep. Estate fees in Lekki and Ikoyi compound quickly. Factor them into your real yield, not just the asking rent.

Who Lagos is best for

Investors with ₦20M+ to deploy, a five- to ten-year horizon, and an appetite for liquidity and rental income. It's the strongest fit for diaspora clients who want dollar-denominated yield through luxury short-lets in Ikoyi, VI, and Lekki Phase 1 — and for buyers who want their wealth visible, liquid, and in motion.

Ibadan: The Balanced Play Most People Sleep On

Ibadan is where I see the highest risk-adjusted returns for most everyday investors today. It's the largest city in West Africa by land mass, it's anchored by the University of Ibadan and a thick research economy, and its land prices are still 20–30% of Lagos equivalents.

What makes Ibadan attractive

  • Affordable entry. You can still acquire prime estate land in Akobo, Jericho, or Alalubosa for ₦5M – ₦15M. A decent four-bedroom duplex sits between ₦60M and ₦120M — Lagos pricing for that quality starts at three to four times higher.
  • Lagos spillover. The Lagos–Ibadan expressway has cut commute time to roughly 2.5 hours on a good day. Remote workers, retirees, and second-home buyers are quietly relocating.
  • University and research economy. UI, LAUTECH extensions, and a growing tech and education cluster create stable, year-round rental demand.
  • Cleaner titles. Oyo State land administration is meaningfully more organized than Lagos. A properly registered Deed of Assignment with ratification is broadly respected, and excisions are easier to verify.

The risks

  • Resale can take three to six months in slow seasons — plan your liquidity accordingly.
  • Some outlying estates have weak infrastructure — internal roads, drainage, and power upgrades are still developing.
  • Buyers negotiate harder than in Lagos. Pricing discipline and patience matter more.

Who Ibadan is best for

First-time buyers, young families building their first home, balanced portfolio investors with ₦5M – ₦50M, and diaspora clients who want exposure without Lagos ticket sizes. This is the entry point I recommend to most clients walking through the door for the first time in 2026.

Where Ogun Sits in This Picture

You'll notice I haven't put Ogun in the table — and that's deliberate. Ogun (specifically the Alabata corridor where I market Paradise Farms) isn't really competing with Lagos or Ibadan as a residential market. It's a different asset class entirely: productive farmland, with a developer-managed yield model, where you're earning from harvests, not from rent or resale.

If you're trying to choose between Lagos and Ibadan, you're choosing between cities. If you're considering Ogun farmland, you're choosing between asset classes. Most of my clients with serious portfolios end up holding a piece of all three — and that's not an accident.

The Verdict: What I Actually Tell Clients

If you have ₦5M – ₦15M

Start with Ibadan estate land. You'll get a clean title, real upside, and leave cash on the side for fencing, a survey plan, or a second plot down the line. This is the quietest way to start a portfolio.

If you have ₦15M – ₦40M

Split across two positions. A plot of Ibadan estate land (₦8M – ₦12M), plus either a Lagos-fringe plot in the Ibeju-Lekki / Epe axis or an Ogun farm plot for cash-flow diversification. You don't have to pick a city — you have to pick a balance.

If you have ₦40M – ₦100M

Anchor in Lagos for rental yield. A one- or two-bedroom serviced flat in Lekki Phase 1 or the Ikoyi fringe, run as a short-let — that's where the dollar income lives. Then add Ibadan land or Ogun farmland for compounding appreciation behind it.

If you have ₦100M+

Build across all three corridors. Lagos for liquidity and rental cash flow. Ibadan for residential and balanced growth. Ogun farmland for productive yield and inflation-hedged appreciation. That's the portfolio shape I help my serious clients put together.

The best city isn't a city. It's the one that matches your season, your capital, and your timeline.

What to Ask Before You Pick a City

  1. How long am I willing to hold? Under three years — Lagos. Three to seven years — Ibadan. Seven to ten — either, with farmland behind it.
  2. Do I want cash flow or capital growth? Cash flow today → Lagos short-let or Ogun farm. Capital growth in five years → Ibadan land.
  3. Am I going to manage this, or do I need someone else to? If you're abroad or busy, lean into managed assets — short-lets with concierge, or farm plots where the developer manages everything end-to-end.
  4. Have I budgeted for the hidden costs? Survey, perfection of title, legal, agency, fencing, service charges. Add 10–15% to whatever the headline price is.
  5. Who is verifying the title? If the answer is "the seller" — walk away. The right answer is an independent lawyer, plus a marketer like me whose reputation rides on the deal closing cleanly.

One Last Thing — A Note on "Cheap"

I see a lot of clients chase the cheapest plot in the cheapest city. Then they spend three years fixing title issues, paying off omo onile, or waiting for roads that never come. Cheap is rarely cheap. The plots I market are not always the lowest priced — they're the ones I can stand behind.

Whichever city you choose, choose the deal that closes cleanly. That's the only rule that matters.
