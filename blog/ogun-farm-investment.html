Ogun State Farm Investment: How Pepper, Tomato, Cucumber & Sweet Potato Plots Pay You Back

By Blessing Collins (Eva) 📅 March 15, 2026 ⏱️ 9 min read

If you've spent any time in Nigerian investment circles in the last few years, you've heard the pitch: "Put in ₦500,000, we'll send you returns from our farm in [insert state]." Some of those promises were honored. Many weren't. A handful collapsed publicly, taking diaspora savings with them.

So when I tell clients about farm investment opportunities, I lead with this: most "agric investment" schemes you've heard of are not real estate — they're loans dressed up in farm clothing. You give money. You hope. The land is not in your name. The crops are not yours. If the operator disappears, you have nothing to repossess.

The opportunity I market is structured differently. It's a real estate deal first and a farm operation second. Here's exactly how it works.

The Three-Part Model (Why This One Doesn't Collapse Like the Others)

Every legitimate farm investment opportunity I represent has three legs. Take any one of them away and I walk.

1. You buy a real plot of land — registered in your name.

Not a "share" in a fund. Not a "unit" in a co-op. A physical plot in Alabata, Odede LGA, Ogun State, surveyed and titled in your name. Even if every farm operation on earth shut down tomorrow, you would still own a piece of Ogun State land that has been appreciating year-over-year.

That single fact eliminates the catastrophic-loss risk that destroyed most "agric investment" platforms. Land doesn't disappear. Operators do.

2. You pay a one-time setup fee — and you know exactly where it goes.

The setup fee covers: land preparation, fencing, drip irrigation system installation, planting, fertilizer and seed inputs, and the labor for the first harvest cycle. It is one-time. It is documented. You receive itemized accounting before you pay it.

This is the part most "agric schemes" hide. Yours will not be hidden.

3. The developer manages the farm — and you receive your share of revenue every harvest cycle.

The on-the-ground operator (developed by Paradise Farms by Agroscale) handles everything: planting, irrigation, pest management, harvest, and sale to local markets. You get periodic updates — photos, videos, and revenue statements — and a transparent share of the harvest income.

Because the crops are short-cycle, harvest happens multiple times per year, not once every 5-7 years like cocoa or palm oil schemes.

What's Grown There — And Why These Crops Specifically

The farm grows four crops on rotation, all chosen for one reason: Nigeria's domestic market consumes them daily, and prices have only gone up.

  • 🌶️ Pepper (rodo, tatashe, chili) — non-negotiable in Nigerian cooking. Domestic demand never softens; price floors keep rising.
  • 🍅 Tomato — Nigeria imports massive volumes of tomato paste because we don't grow enough fresh fruit. Local tomato is in constant short supply during off-season.
  • 🥒 Cucumber — high yield per square meter, short cycle (~50-60 days), and demand has tripled in the last decade thanks to wellness and salad culture in urban centers.
  • 🍠 Sweet Potato — staple, storable, and increasingly popular as a healthier carbohydrate alternative.

Notice what's not on this list: cassava, oil palm, cashew, cocoa. Those are multi-year crops. They tie up your money for half a decade before the first cup of returns. Short-cycle crops are how you get paid more than once per year.

Why Alabata, Specifically?

The location matters more than most people realize. Alabata sits in Odede LGA, Ogun State — about 90 minutes from central Lagos depending on traffic. That proximity is the entire game.

  • Same-day market access. Harvest in the morning, on Lagos market shelves by afternoon. No spoilage from long-haul transport.
  • Land prices still reasonable. Compared to land in Lekki or Ibeju-Lekki, Alabata farm plots are a fraction of the price — but the underlying land continues to appreciate as Lagos sprawl expands westward.
  • Water access & soil quality. Ogun State sits in the rain forest belt; reliable rainfall plus drip irrigation means crops grow year-round.
  • Government support for agriculture. Ogun State has one of Nigeria's most active agricultural development frameworks. Less bureaucratic friction, fewer surprises.

Every farm intake has different pricing depending on plot size and current setup costs. I won't quote a number publicly because (a) the prices change quarterly, and (b) I'd rather walk you through the live pricing on WhatsApp where I can also show you the documentation.

What I can share publicly:

  • You receive your share of revenue per cycle, not annually — meaning multiple distributions per year.
  • The land underneath you is permanent capital. If at any point you decide farming isn't for you, you still own the plot and can resell at appreciated value.
  • Diaspora investors typically buy 2-3 plots and let the revenue compound by reinvesting into additional plots over time.

Who This Is For — And Who It Isn't

This is right for you if:

  • You want a tangible Nigerian asset — something you can drive to and stand on.
  • You like the idea of passive farm revenue without having to manage anything yourself.
  • You're a diaspora investor who wants Nigerian exposure but also wants to own the underlying land, not just hold a paper claim.
  • You want to build a portfolio of multiple plots over 2-3 years.

This is wrong for you if:

  • You expect overnight returns. Farms grow at the pace of nature, not crypto markets.
  • You're allergic to documentation. There is paperwork — survey plans, deed of assignment, setup-fee receipts, revenue statements. If that bores you, this isn't your investment.
  • You can't afford to leave the setup fee tied up for at least 12 months. (Not because the farm fails — but because farming has cycles.)

What to Ask Before You Commit (Even If You're Working With Me)

  1. Show me the survey plan and title path for the specific plot I'd own.
  2. Itemize the setup fee — what specifically does each line cover?
  3. How are revenues calculated and verified — and what cadence will I receive distributions?
  4. Who manages disputes if a harvest underperforms or weather affects the cycle?
  5. Can I visit the plot, and what's the protocol if I can't fly in (diaspora video tour, etc.)?

Any farm marketer who flinches at those questions is not someone you should be working with. (I welcome them — they're the only way you confirm I'm worth your trust.)

Final Word

This isn't a get-rich-quick story. Farming is one of the oldest forms of wealth on the planet, and short-cycle crop investment in Nigeria is having its moment because: (a) the population keeps growing, (b) imports keep getting more expensive with every naira-dollar move, and (c) tech-enabled drip-irrigation farming makes per-plot yields finally make sense.
